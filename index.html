MY NAME IS RADHA

A Story of Purity, Pretense, and Film by Saadat Hasan Manto
Enter The Studio

Bombay, 1939

The Undisputed Master

Saadat Hasan Manto (1912 – 1955) has been called the greatest short story writer of the Indian subcontinent. Born in Punjab, he navigated the chaotic, vibrant world of Bombay as a radio and film scriptwriter, journalist, and author.

His stories were highly controversial, unafraid to look into the dark, marginalized corners of society. He was tried for obscenity six times during his career—a testament to his refusal to sanitize reality.

Manto's world in Bombay was crowded with Hindus, Sikhs, and Muslims; with hookers and heiresses, clerks and movie stars. He wrote of their lives with profound empathy and a sharp, cynical wit. After the tragic violence of Partition, he moved to Lahore, where the separation from his beloved Bombay eventually broke him.

1930s Bombay Cinema

In the late 1930s, the Bombay film industry was a chaotic blend of art, commerce, and survival. B-grade studios churned out films like "The Beauty of the Forest" while scriptwriters earned a meager forty rupees a month.

The studios were melting pots. You had 'pure' leading men draped in khadi cloth, professing nationalistic ideals while measuring their biceps. And you had women like Neelam—the daughter of a Banarasi prostitute—brought in to play the 'vamp' because polite society refused to acknowledge them as anything else.

Manto captures the hypocrisy of this era perfectly. The men who played saints on screen often lacked the raw, unapologetic honesty of the women they looked down upon. When the facade cracks, as it does in "My Name is Radha", the truth is violent, bloody, and entirely real.
